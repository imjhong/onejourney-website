Understanding the Post-Cremation Ceremonies

The mourning period in Hindu traditions lasts for thirteen days, marked by sacred rituals that help the departed soul on its journey and provide comfort to the family. We are here to help you conduct these ceremonies with reverence and peace.

The Significance of the Chautha (Fourth Day)

The Chautha ceremony, held on the fourth day after death, is a time for close family and relatives to gather for prayers (Shanti Path) and spiritual discourse (Geeta Pravachan). It is a solemn occasion to pray for the peace of the departed soul and to support one another in grief.

The Importance of the Tehravin (Thirteenth Day)

The Tehravin marks the end of the primary mourning period. It is believed that on this day, the soul severs its earthly ties and begins its journey onward. The ceremony involves a Havan or Puja, a Brahman Bhoj to seek blessings, and a feast for the community, signifying the family's return to normal life.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main difference between Chautha and Tehravin?

+

The Chautha is the first gathering of mourning on the fourth day, primarily for prayers. The Tehravin is the final ceremony on the thirteenth day that concludes the mourning period and includes a community feast.

Which areas in Delhi NCR do you cover?

Can we customize the catering menu for the Tehravin?

Do we need to arrange for the pandit and samagri ourselves?
